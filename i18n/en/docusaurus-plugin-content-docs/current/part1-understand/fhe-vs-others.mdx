# FHE vs ZK vs MPC: What Web3 Developers Need to Know

> If you already understand Zero-Knowledge Proofs (ZK), this article helps you establish a clear boundary between FHE and ZK/MPC. This is the most worthwhile 10 minutes to spend before entering FHE development.

## Start with the Conclusion

These three technologies solve **fundamentally different problems**. They are not competing but complementary:

| Technology | Core Capability | Solves What Problem |
|------|----------|-------------|
| **ZK (Zero-Knowledge Proof)** | Prove "I know something" without revealing details | **Verifiability**: Prove computation is correct but hide inputs |
| **FHE (Fully Homomorphic Encryption)** | Directly compute on ciphertext, result is still ciphertext | **Computational Privacy**: Third party can participate in computation but cannot see data |
| **MPC (Secure Multi-Party Computation)** | Multiple parties jointly compute, each only knows their own input | **Collaborative Computation**: Multiple mutually distrusting participants compute jointly |

---

## What ZK Solves vs What FHE Solves

This is the most common confusion point for Web3 developers. An analogy:

> **ZK is "presenting a certificate"**: You prove to a bank that your income exceeds a threshold, but don't tell the bank the specific amount. The bank can verify your conclusion is correct.
>
> **FHE is "delegating computation"**: You hand your encrypted financial data to an accounting firm to calculate your taxes for you, but they can't see your numbers throughout the entire process — they can only return the result to you.

In more technical language:

- **ZK**: Prover knows `x` such that `f(x) = y`, without revealing `x`. Verifier only confirms `y` and proof validity
- **FHE**: Computing party, without knowing `x`, directly computes `Enc(f(x))`, and the result is returned to the data owner for decryption

## In Web3, When to Use Which?

### Scenarios for Using ZK

- **Proving state correctness** (core of rollup): Prove that off-chain executed n transactions and state transitions are legal
- **Proving identity/qualification** (zkKYC): Prove you are > 18 without revealing birthday
- **Proving possession**: Prove you hold an NFT without revealing wallet address

ZK's keyword is **"prove"** — you want others to believe something is true without revealing more information.

### Scenarios for Using FHE

- **Private state computation**: On-chain contracts need to add/subtract encrypted balances, but no one (including nodes) can see the specific numbers
- **Sealed auction**: Everyone submits encrypted bids, contract determines the highest price without decryption
- **Private voting**: Contract counts votes without revealing who voted for whom
- **Off-chain private AI**: Run machine learning models without exposing user data

FHE's keyword is **"delegate computation"** — you have others compute for you but don't trust them with your data.

### They Can Be Combined

In fhEVM's implementation, ZK and FHE actually work together:

- When users submit encrypted inputs, they need to attach a **ZK proof** (ZKPoK) proving they know the plaintext corresponding to the ciphertext
- FHE is responsible for **executing encrypted computation** on-chain/coprocessor
- Final results are securely restored among multiple nodes through **MPC (threshold decryption)**

---

## Where MPC Fits

MPC typically appears in two scenarios:

1. **Key management**: FHE private keys cannot be held by a single point (otherwise that node can decrypt everything). In fhEVM, private keys are sharded and stored across multiple key management nodes via MPC, and decryption requires threshold数量的 nodes to cooperate
2. **Multi-party joint computation**: Multiple institutions (like multiple banks) each own partial data, use MPC for joint computation without leaking raw data to each other

The difference between MPC and FHE: MPC usually requires multiple parties to be **simultaneously online** with multiple rounds of communication, while FHE allows **asynchronous single-party computation**, more suitable for blockchain's asynchronous environment.

## Performance Comparison (Rough Reference)

| Technology | Computation Cost | Communication Cost | Data Size Expansion |
|------|----------|----------|-------------|
| ZK | Medium (slow proof generation, fast verification) | Low | Low |
| FHE | High (1000x~100000x slower than plaintext) | Low (asynchronous, no multi-round) | High (ciphertext much larger than plaintext) |
| MPC | Medium | High (multi-round interaction) | Low |

> **tip** FHE's computation cost is the main bottleneck currently, but specialized hardware (GPU acceleration, ASIC) and Coprocessor architecture are rapidly improving this.

## Quick Decision Table

```
What is your need?
│
├── I need to prove something to others is true, without revealing more → ZK
│
├── I need others to help me compute, but cannot let them see my data → FHE
│
├── Multiple mutually distrusting institutions need to jointly compute a result → MPC
│
└── I need to perform business logic on encrypted user data on-chain → FHE (fhEVM)
```
